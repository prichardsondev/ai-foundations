Big picture reference

Categories of AI models

AI is not one single thing. Different model types are built for different jobs: chatting, writing, image creation, vision, detection, speech, recommendations, forecasting, robotics, and more.

What you should take away

  • Not all AI models do the same kind of work
  • GPT-style chat models are only one category
  • Vision, speech, detection, and forecasting models matter in the real world too
  • The best model depends on the job, the data, and the risk level

1. The simplest way to think about AI models

Ask one question first: what kind of input goes in, and what kind of output needs to come out?

Useful mental model

Text in, text out often means a language model.

Image in, labels or boxes out often means a vision or detection model.

Audio in, text out often means speech recognition.

Numbers in, future values out often means a prediction or forecasting model.

2. Major categories of AI models

These are the categories most people are likely to hear about or encounter at work.

Large language models

What they do: Work with language — answering questions, summarizing, drafting, explaining, and rewriting.

Examples: GPT models, Claude, Grok, Gemini, Llama.

Common uses: Email drafts, reports, SOP summaries, research help, chatbots, coding support.

Image generation models

What they do: Create new images from prompts or other images.

Examples: DALL·E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, Flux.

Common uses: Marketing concepts, design mockups, storyboards, posters, product ideas.

Computer vision classification models

What they do: Look at an image and decide what is in it.

Examples: ResNet-style classifiers, Vision Transformers.

Common uses: Defect detection, medical image support, sorting, quality control, species or object recognition.

Object detection models

What they do: Find objects in an image and mark where they are.

Examples: YOLO, Faster R-CNN, SSD.

Common uses: Safety monitoring, security cameras, traffic systems, warehouse counting, manufacturing inspection.

Segmentation models

What they do: Separate an image into detailed regions or exact object shapes.

Examples: U-Net, Mask R-CNN, Segment Anything Model (SAM).

Common uses: Medical imaging, image editing, robotics vision, precision inspection, floor-plan or map analysis.

Speech and audio models

What they do: Convert speech to text, text to speech, or identify patterns in audio.

Examples: Whisper, wav2vec, modern TTS models.

Common uses: Transcription, call analysis, voice assistants, captions, spoken interfaces.

Recommendation models

What they do: Suggest the next item, product, video, song, or action.

Examples: Collaborative filtering systems, ranking models.

Common uses: Streaming platforms, shopping suggestions, internal knowledge ranking, search relevance.

Prediction and forecasting models

What they do: Predict a future value, class, failure, or trend from structured data.

Examples: XGBoost, random forests, neural networks, time-series models.

Common uses: Demand forecasting, maintenance prediction, fraud detection, energy usage, staffing estimates.

3. Popular model names people hear a lot

These names come up often, but they do not all belong to the same category.

GPT

A family of generative language models used for conversation, writing, reasoning, and coding help.

Claude

A family of language models often used for writing, analysis, and long-document work.

Grok

A chat-oriented language model used for question answering and general assistant tasks.

YOLO

A fast object detection family known for real-time computer vision tasks.

SAM

A segmentation model family designed to outline and isolate objects in images.

Whisper

A speech recognition model used to turn audio into text.

4. Where these models show up in real life

Most workplaces will encounter more than one category over time.

Office and knowledge work

  • LLMs for drafting, summarizing, brainstorming, and document help
  • Speech models for meetings and transcription
  • Recommendation or ranking models for search and knowledge retrieval

Manufacturing and field operations

  • Detection models for part counting and safety monitoring
  • Vision and segmentation models for defects and inspection
  • Forecasting models for maintenance, downtime, and inventory

Healthcare and diagnostics

  • Vision models for image review support
  • Language models for documentation and summarization
  • Prediction models for risk scoring and decision support

Consumer products

  • Chatbots and assistants in apps and devices
  • Speech models in phones and smart speakers
  • Recommendation engines in shopping and streaming

5. One important reminder

A popular model is not automatically the right model.

Good model selection starts with the job

If the goal is to draft an email, a language model makes sense. If the goal is to find forklifts or people in a camera feed, use a detection model. If the goal is to predict next month’s energy usage, use a forecasting model. The smartest-looking model is not always the best fit.

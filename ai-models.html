Big picture reference

Categories of AI models

AI is not one single thing. There are many different types of AI models, each built for specific jobs like chatting, creating images, understanding photos, predicting trends, and more.

Understanding AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

These three terms are used everywhere — here’s the simplest way to understand them and how they relate.

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

The broad goal: building machines that can do tasks that normally require human intelligence — such as understanding language, recognizing objects, making decisions, or solving problems.

Machine Learning (ML)

The main way we achieve AI today. Instead of programming every rule manually, we give the computer lots of examples (data) and let it learn patterns by itself.

Deep Learning (DL)

A powerful type of Machine Learning that uses artificial neural networks with many layers. It is especially good at handling complex data like images, audio, video, and natural language.

How they relate

AI is the big umbrella goal
  ↓
Machine Learning (ML) is the primary approach we use
  ↓
Deep Learning (DL) is the most powerful technique inside ML

Simple analogy: AI is the house we want to build. Machine Learning is the main construction method. Deep Learning is the advanced power tool that makes difficult tasks (like understanding photos or conversations) much easier and more effective.

What you should take away

  • Not all AI models do the same kind of work
  • Chat models (like GPT) are only one category of AI
  • Vision, speech, detection, and forecasting models are very common in the real world
  • The best model always depends on the specific job, the data available, and the level of risk

1. The simplest way to think about AI models

Start with one simple question: What kind of input goes in, and what kind of output needs to come out?

Useful mental model

Text in → Text out → usually a language model

Image in → Labels or boxes out → usually a vision or detection model

Audio in → Text out → usually a speech recognition model

Numbers in → Future values out → usually a forecasting or prediction model

2. Major categories of AI models

These are the types you’re most likely to hear about or use in the workplace.

Large language models

What they do: Work with language — answering questions, summarizing, drafting, explaining, and rewriting.

Examples: GPT models, Claude, Grok, Gemini, Llama.

Common uses: Email drafts, reports, summaries, research help, chatbots, coding support.

Image generation models

What they do: Create new images from text prompts or reference images.

Examples: DALL·E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, Flux.

Common uses: Marketing visuals, design mockups, storyboards, posters, product concepts.

Computer vision classification models

What they do: Look at an image and decide what it contains.

Examples: ResNet-style classifiers, Vision Transformers.

Common uses: Quality control, medical image support, sorting, object recognition.

Object detection models

What they do: Find and locate specific objects inside images or videos.

Examples: YOLO, Faster R-CNN, SSD.

Common uses: Safety monitoring, security cameras, traffic systems, warehouse automation.

Segmentation models

What they do: Outline exact shapes and boundaries of objects in an image.

Examples: U-Net, Mask R-CNN, Segment Anything Model (SAM).

Common uses: Medical imaging, photo editing, robotics, precision manufacturing.

Speech and audio models

What they do: Convert speech to text, text to speech, or analyze audio patterns.

Examples: Whisper, modern text-to-speech models.

Common uses: Meeting transcription, voice assistants, captions, call analysis.

Recommendation models

What they do: Suggest the next item, product, video, or action.

Examples: Collaborative filtering and ranking systems.

Common uses: Streaming services, e-commerce suggestions, internal search ranking.

Prediction and forecasting models

What they do: Predict future values or trends from structured data.

Examples: XGBoost, random forests, time-series models.

Common uses: Demand forecasting, maintenance prediction, fraud detection, staffing estimates.

3. Popular model names people hear a lot

These names come up often, but they belong to different categories.

GPT

A family of generative language models used for conversation, writing, reasoning, and coding help.

Claude

A family of language models known for careful writing, analysis, and long-document work.

Grok

A helpful chat-oriented language model for answering questions and general assistance.

YOLO

A fast and popular family of object detection models for real-time vision tasks.

SAM

The Segment Anything Model — great at outlining objects in images.

Whisper

A powerful speech recognition model that turns audio into text.

4. Where these models show up in real life

Most organizations use several different types of AI models.

Office and knowledge work

  • Language models for drafting, summarizing, and brainstorming
  • Speech models for transcription and meeting notes
  • Recommendation models for better search and knowledge discovery

Manufacturing and operations

  • Detection and vision models for safety and quality inspection
  • Forecasting models for inventory and maintenance planning

Healthcare

  • Vision models to support medical image review
  • Language models for documentation and patient summaries
  • Prediction models for risk assessment

Consumer apps and products

  • Chatbots and virtual assistants
  • Speech recognition in phones and smart devices
  • Recommendation engines in shopping and entertainment

5. Important reminder

Choose the right model for the job

The most popular or “smartest” model is not always the best one. Start with the task: What input do you have? What output do you need? Then pick the model that fits best.

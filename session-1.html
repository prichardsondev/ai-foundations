1. What AI actually is

AI tools are good at predicting useful language and patterns from your input, but they are not judgment, common sense, or truth.

Key idea

AI is not magic. It is software that learned patterns from training data and is often good at predicting useful responses, but that does not mean it understands situations the way people do.

Three things to know

An AI tool runs on a model — a system trained to recognize patterns in language.

Training produces learned values (sometimes called parameters) — the adjustments that shape how the model responds to any given input.

When you send a prompt, the model uses those learned values to generate a response. That process is called inference.

What a model is

A model is the part of the AI system that has learned patterns from a very large amount of example data. In plain English, it is software that has been trained to produce useful responses — not a search engine, not a database, and not a person.

How a model learns

During training, the model works through enormous amounts of text and fine-tunes its internal patterns — its parameters — until it becomes reliable at predicting useful responses.

Think of it like a very well-read assistant who has absorbed millions of examples. It does not look things up in real time. It draws on what it learned during training.

Inference

Inference is using the model to generate an answer from input. In everyday terms, it is the moment when your prompt goes in and the model produces a response.

2. What a GPT is

GPT is a useful label because each part of the name helps explain how the tool works and why it behaves the way it does.

Generative

It creates new text based on patterns it learned. It does not simply retrieve one stored answer.

Pre-trained

It learned from large amounts of data before you ever started using it.

Transformer

It reads your full conversation — not just your last message — to produce a more relevant response. That is why adding context to a prompt usually improves the answer.

What GPT tools are good at

  • Drafting emails, updates, and summaries
  • Explaining concepts in plain language
  • Reorganizing or reformatting your text
  • Generating options when you are stuck

Where GPT tools fall short

  • They can state wrong facts with full confidence
  • They do not know your company, team, or situation unless you tell them
  • They have a training cutoff and may not know recent information
  • They cannot verify whether what they produce is accurate

What "confident but wrong" looks like

If you ask a GPT tool for a specific regulation, policy number, or fact it was not trained on, it may produce a plausible-sounding answer that is completely made up. For example, it might cite "OSHA regulation 29 CFR 1910.278" — a number that does not exist — with complete confidence. This is called a hallucination. It looks like a real answer — but it is not.

3. Prompt engineering basics

A better prompt gives the tool a clearer job, clearer context, and a clearer target for the answer.

Simple prompt structure

Role: who the AI should act like

Task: what you want it to do

Context: the details it needs

Constraints: limits, tone, length, or rules

Output format: how the answer should be presented

Why this matters

When prompts are vague, the tool fills in the blanks on its own. When prompts are clear, the output is usually more useful and easier to verify.

Bad prompt

Weak example

Help with this email.

Too vague. The AI has to guess the audience, tone, details, and format.

Better prompt

Clearer example

Write a short professional email telling my team that the meeting moved from Thursday at 2 PM to Friday at 10 AM.

Better, because it gives the task, audience, and key change.

Best prompt

Structured example

Role: Professional workplace communication assistant
Task: Draft an email to my team about a schedule change
Context: The meeting originally scheduled for Thursday at 2 PM is now Friday at 10 AM
Constraints: Keep it friendly, clear, and under 120 words
Output format: Email with subject line and body

Best, because it gives the AI a role, a task, the key facts, clear limits, and a target format.

Role: Training assistant
Task: Summarize this SOP for new employees
Context: Forklift battery change basic steps:
Park forklift in battery area
Turn off machine
Put on gloves and face shield
Open battery compartment carefully
Disconnect old battery
Use lift tool to remove battery
Put in charged battery and reconnect
Check connection before starting forklift
If battery looks damaged tell supervisor
Constraints: Use plain English. Focus on the most important safety and process steps. Keep it under 8 bullet points.
Output format: Bullet list

        

        

          
Workplace example: Supervisor update

          
Role: Professional writing assistant
Task: Draft a short update for my supervisor
Context: I need to explain that shipment delays affected this week's schedule
Constraints: Keep it clear, direct, and under 150 words
Output format: Short email

        

      


      

        
Workplace reminder

        
Even with a strong prompt, always review the output before using it. Check facts, names, dates, and anything sensitive before you send or share it.

      


      

        
Practical tip

        
AI often works better when you bring rough notes, draft instructions, or plain workplace text for the tool to improve. That is usually easier and more realistic than starting from a blank page.
